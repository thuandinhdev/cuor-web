Breath of Living Spaces, Shape of Future, One Simulation at a Time

Natural ventilation analysis using CFD simulation

The Emerald 68 building has been remodeled for investigating its natural ventilation in relation to local wind directions. The evaluation methodology was based on the Building and Construction Authority's (BCA) Green Mark (GM) Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) analysis from 2021. This paper aims to provide project investors with essential insights for evaluating and enhancing natural ventilation quality in buildings. However, it's important to note that this analysis was based on a reconstructed building model derived from estimations available on the website XXX. Therefore, the results only represent a partial assessment of the building's natural ventilation capabilities.

Summary

The Residential Building Criteria requires that units with sufficient natural ventilation have to achieve a weight-average area wind speed of 0.6 m/s and more.

The findings indicated that only apartment A02 approximately attained the benchmark for adequate natural ventilation among the 12 apartments on the typical floor. Apartments A11 and A12 exhibited velocities much below the threshold for adequate natural ventilation.

Modeling

The apartments were built based on images from the website of project X.

The computational domain shall encompass the area of interest, the attributes of the local environment, and the buildings located within a distance of at least three times the length of the longest border measurement of the development.

Topographical information must be incorporated into the simulation models if the building and adjacent development are situated in hilly terrain. The computational domain will be expanded from the outer edge of the proximity regions to the border to avoid non-physical airflow solutions when convergence is achieved.

The computational domain must be oriented in accordance with the direction of wind flow. The domain height will be increased to about three times the height of the highest building in the designated area.

Methodology

IThe simulation of a green building in OpenFOAM uses a computational fluid dynamics (CFD) approach based on the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations

The equations are solved in a 3D domain (x, y, z) to describe the flow of air around the building and its surrounding environment. The simulation assumes that the flow is:

  • Incompressible: The density of the air remains constant, which is a reasonable assumption for most building applications.
  • Turbulent: The flow is characterized by chaotic and unpredictable fluctuations, as it is typically the case for air movement around buildings.
  • Steady-state: The flow conditions are considered to be unchanging over time, although this can be modified to account for varying wind conditions.

The OpenFOAM software utilizes a finite volume method to solve the RANS equations. These equations, along with the standard k-epsilon turbulence model, form the foundation of the simulation.

The SIMPLE (Semi-Implicit Method for Pressure-Linked Equations) algorithm is implemented within OpenFOAM's simpleFoam solver. This algorithm is used to solve the coupled equations for pressure and velocity, which are essential for accurately describing the fluid flow field.
